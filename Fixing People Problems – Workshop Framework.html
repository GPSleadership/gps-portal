People Problem
Decision Guide

A simple framework to choose the right fix before you spend time and money
Who This Is For

CEOs and owners of multi-location, operations-heavy businesses whose stores are full, phones are ringing, and people work hard… but projects still stall, decisions are slow, and the same headaches keep coming back.

You have a strong team on paper and solid customer demand. This framework helps you pinpoint the real leadership bottlenecks and choose the right fixes before you pour more time and money into the wrong solutions.

STEP 1
ASSESS

Where Is This Problem Really Coming From?

Use this when you recognize your business in one of the scenarios from the workshop. Before you spend time or money on a solution, slow down and answer these.
Q1

What's actually happening?

  • Describe the situation in one or two sentences.
  • Where do you see it show up: customers, staff, or results?
Example: "Our new regional manager is buried in day-to-day problems from multiple stores and isn't leading other leaders."
Your notes
Q2

Why is this a real business problem (not just annoying)?

  • Customers — uptime, responsiveness, experience
  • Your people — morale, turnover risk
  • Your numbers — revenue, margin, rework, delays
If you can't tie it to customers, people, or numbers, it may not be your top priority right now.
Your notes
Q3

How big is this compared to your other issues?

  • On a scale of 1–10, how urgent is it to make progress on this in the next 12 months?
  • What would happen if you did nothing for the next year?
Some problems are loud but small. Others are quiet but expensive.
Your notes
Q4

What do you believe is driving it? Check what seems most true right now:

Lack of clarity (unclear expectations, roles, or success criteria)
Lack of support (not enough time, resources, or authority)
Lack of skill (leader hasn't been taught how to lead at this level)
Structural issue (wrong person, wrong org design, or wrong incentive)
Culture / behavior (people have learned to wait, avoid conflict, or say "yes" and do nothing)
You don't need perfect certainty. You just need a best first guess so you don't throw random solutions at the wall.
The GPS Insight

"If you can't describe the problem clearly, you can't buy or design the right solution. Most wasted training budgets come from skipping Step 1."

STEP 2
DELIVER

Design the Right Response — Not "Training by Default"

Once you've named the problem, its impact, and your best guess at the cause, choose the type of response before you pick the specific vendor, course, or program.
① Is this a clarity problem? If yes, you probably need:
  • Clearer expectations and non-negotiables
  • A simple playbook / checklist
  • Better communication of "how we do it here"
② Is this a support / capacity problem? If yes, you probably need:
  • To remove competing priorities
  • To adjust workload, headcount, or tools
  • Your own behavior change (what you stop doing for them)
③ Is this a skill / behavior problem? If yes, you probably need:
  • Targeted coaching or mentoring
  • Focused training on 1–2 critical behaviors
  • Practice and feedback, not a one-time event
④ Is this a structure / fit problem? If yes, you probably need:
  • Role redesign
  • Different reporting lines or decision rights
  • In some cases, a tough call on whether this is the right person for this role
Best Practice Questions for Deliver
  • What is the smallest, sharpest intervention we could try for 30–90 days?
  • Can this be handled internally, or do we need outside help?
  • Who needs to be part of designing the solution so they'll actually own it?
STEP 3
SUSTAIN

Make Sure It Doesn't Become "Flavor of the Month"

Good ideas die when there is no plan to track them, talk about them, or adjust them.
1
Review It on a Rhythm

Add one line to an existing weekly or bi-weekly meeting: "What did we commit to change last time, and what happened?" Keep this to 5 minutes. Every time.

2
Name the Drift Early

Expect leaders to slide back toward old habits. When you see it, call it out quickly and calmly:

"We agreed you would handle X this way. What got in the way?"
3
Measure What Matters

Decide up front how you'll know it's working: fewer repeat issues from the same store or leader, faster response times for customers, reduced rework or callbacks. Review these numbers every 30 days for at least one quarter.
