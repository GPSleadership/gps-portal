Who This Is For Leaders who communicate well most of the time — but need a system for when they don't. Use this as a pre-conversation checklist, a post-conflict debrief tool, or a 90-day practice guide.

Core Principles Under Pressure
Principle 1
Emotion Is Information
Your emotional reaction is a signal, not a verdict. Acknowledge what you're feeling before you speak. Name the signal; don't broadcast it.

Principle 2
Separate People From Behavior
Address what happened — specifically and recently. Avoid patterns ("you always"), labels ("you're defensive"), or character judgments.

Principle 3
Go Slower to Go Faster
The two-minute pause costs less than the two-week fallout. Slow down when your instinct is to escalate. Speed kills clarity.

Principle 4
Lead With Shared Mission
Anchor hard conversations to what you're both trying to accomplish. When you disagree on approach, return to the outcome you share.

Writing Difficult Emails
Before You Hit Send
Ask yourself:
  • Would I say this out loud to their face?
  • Is this email defensive or constructive?
  • Is a 30-minute conversation better?
If the answer to the last one is yes — pick up the phone.
1
State Your Purpose
One sentence. Why are you writing and what do you need from the reader?
2
Describe the Situation
Facts only — what happened, when, and who was involved. No interpretation yet.
3
Share the Impact
What effect did this have on the work, the team, or the outcome? Keep it observable.
4
Make a Clear Request
What specific action do you want? By when? Ambiguity creates more email.
5
Invite a Response
"If you see this differently, I'd like to hear it." Leaves the door open without hedging your point.

GPS Insight
Most communication breakdowns aren't caused by what leaders say — they're caused by what leaders skip. The pause, the question, the restatement. The discipline to slow down when pressure goes up is a leadership skill, not a personality trait. It can be trained.
